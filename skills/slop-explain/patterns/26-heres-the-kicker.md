# Pattern 26: "Here's the kicker"

**False-suspense transitions that promise revelations before unremarkable observations: "Here's the thing," "Here's where it gets interesting," "Here's what most people miss."**

## Why LLMs do this

The form is common in opinion writing, Substack essays, and motivational content. All are heavily represented in training data. The transitions create a beat of anticipation before a claim, which gives the claim more apparent weight than it would have on its own.

The model defaults to these transitions whenever it wants to mark a sentence as important. Because the model has weak commitments about what is actually important, it deploys the transitions broadly, including before claims that are not surprising at all.

## Why it reads as AI

The promise rarely cashes out. "Here's the kicker: it was never about the technology." If the surrounding paragraph already implied the point, the kicker reveals nothing. The reader feels the buildup and then the deflation, and learns to discount any sentence that begins with this scaffolding.

The forms also cluster. AI essays often have three or four "Here's the X" sentences in a single piece, which makes each one feel scripted. Real writers use the construction sparingly because they know it cheapens if overused.

The pattern overlaps with rhetorical Q&A (pattern 16). Both stage a reveal that the writer did not actually earn.

## Examples

After:
> It was never about the technology.

Before:
> Here's the kicker: it was never about the technology.

After:
> Most people focus on the wrong metric.

Before:
> Here's what most people miss: they focus on the wrong metric.

After:
> The cost of context switching is the real bottleneck.

Before:
> Here's where it gets interesting: the cost of context switching is the real bottleneck.

Notice that the human versions assert the point directly. The AI versions perform suspense before delivering a claim that did not need a runway.

## How to self-spot

Search for the trigger phrases: "Here's the thing," "Here's the kicker," "Here's what most people miss," "Here's where it gets interesting," "But here's the real question," "And here's the surprise."

For each hit, drop the scaffolding and let the next sentence stand. If the next sentence is not strong enough to stand on its own, strengthen it. Do not strengthen the scaffolding.

The construction is acceptable maybe once per long piece, used genuinely for emphasis. More than that becomes a tic.
